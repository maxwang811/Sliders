# Randomized trial of drug alphaxolin in moderate hypertension

- Trial ID: SAMPLE-HTX-001
- Status: Example document for SLIDERS. Not a real study.

## Abstract

**Background.** Alphaxolin is an investigational selective vasodilator proposed for the management of moderate essential hypertension in adults who remain above target on monotherapy.

**Methods.** This was a double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group randomized trial conducted at 12 outpatient cardiology centers. Adults aged 40-70 years with sustained seated systolic blood pressure between 140 and 160 mmHg on a single background antihypertensive agent were randomly assigned in a 1:1 ratio to receive oral alphaxolin 20 mg once daily or matching placebo for 12 weeks. The primary endpoint was change from baseline in mean 24-hour ambulatory systolic blood pressure at week 12.

**Results.** A total of 420 participants were randomized (210 per arm). Mean age was 58.4 years and 46% were female. At week 12, the adjusted mean change in 24-hour systolic blood pressure was −8.1 mmHg (95% CI −9.6 to −6.6) in the alphaxolin group and −2.3 mmHg (95% CI −3.8 to −0.8) in the placebo group, for a between-group difference of −5.8 mmHg (P < 0.001). Treatment-emergent adverse events occurred in 18% of alphaxolin and 15% of placebo participants. Three serious adverse events occurred in the alphaxolin group and two in the placebo group; none were judged related to study drug.

**Conclusions.** Alphaxolin added to background antihypertensive therapy produced a clinically meaningful reduction in 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure over 12 weeks with an acceptable safety profile.

## Methods

### Design
Multicenter, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group randomized trial. Participants, investigators, and outcome adjudicators were masked to treatment assignment.

### Participants
Eligible participants were adults 40-70 years of age with seated systolic BP 140-160 mmHg on a stable dose of a single first-line antihypertensive (ACE inhibitor, ARB, calcium channel blocker, or thiazide) for at least eight weeks prior to enrollment. Key exclusions were secondary hypertension, prior cardiovascular event within six months, eGFR < 45 mL/min/1.73m², and pregnancy.

### Intervention
Alphaxolin 20 mg orally once daily, or matching placebo, for 12 weeks, added to the participant's existing antihypertensive regimen.

### Outcomes
The primary outcome was the change from baseline in mean 24-hour ambulatory systolic blood pressure at week 12. Secondary outcomes included change in office systolic blood pressure, change in diastolic blood pressure, and safety assessed by treatment-emergent adverse events.

## Results

### Participants
Between March 2024 and August 2024, 512 participants were screened and 420 were randomized (210 alphaxolin, 210 placebo). Baseline characteristics were balanced. Of randomized participants, 198 in the alphaxolin group and 202 in the placebo group completed week 12.

### Primary outcome
Adjusted mean change in 24-hour ambulatory systolic BP at week 12 was −8.1 mmHg in the alphaxolin group and −2.3 mmHg in the placebo group; between-group difference −5.8 mmHg (95% CI −7.5 to −4.1; P < 0.001).

### Secondary outcomes
Office systolic BP decreased by −9.4 mmHg (alphaxolin) vs −3.1 mmHg (placebo). Office diastolic BP decreased by −5.2 mmHg vs −1.6 mmHg.

### Adverse events
Treatment-emergent adverse events were reported in 37/210 (18%) alphaxolin and 32/210 (15%) placebo participants. The most common events in the alphaxolin group were headache (6%), dizziness (4%), and peripheral edema (3%).

## Discussion

In adults with moderate hypertension uncontrolled on monotherapy, adding alphaxolin reduced 24-hour ambulatory systolic blood pressure by approximately 6 mmHg more than placebo over 12 weeks. The effect was consistent across prespecified subgroups including age, sex, baseline background therapy class, and presence of diabetes.

The trial has several limitations. The 12-week duration is too short to assess cardiovascular outcomes. Background therapy was restricted to monotherapy, so the findings may not generalize to patients on multiple antihypertensives. The study population was predominantly from high-income countries.

## Conclusion

Alphaxolin 20 mg daily added to background antihypertensive therapy produced a clinically meaningful reduction in 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure with an acceptable short-term safety profile. Longer trials powered on cardiovascular outcomes are warranted before routine use can be recommended.

# Phase II trial of betapril in heart failure with preserved ejection fraction

- Trial ID: SAMPLE-HFPEF-004
- Status: Example document for SLIDERS. Not a real study.

## Abstract

**Background.** Betapril is a novel small-molecule natriuretic peptide potentiator being developed for heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF), a condition with limited pharmacological options.

**Methods.** We conducted a Phase II, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled dose-finding trial at 18 academic medical centers. Patients with symptomatic HFpEF (LVEF ≥ 50%, NYHA class II-III, elevated NT-proBNP) were randomized 1:1:1:1 to betapril 40 mg, betapril 80 mg, betapril 160 mg, or placebo, all once daily for 24 weeks. The primary endpoint was change from baseline in NT-proBNP at week 24.

**Results.** We randomized 284 participants (mean age 68.2 years, 54% female, median baseline NT-proBNP 912 pg/mL). At week 24, the geometric mean ratio of NT-proBNP versus placebo was 0.92 (95% CI 0.83-1.02) for the 40 mg arm, 0.81 (0.72-0.91) for the 80 mg arm, and 0.78 (0.70-0.88) for the 160 mg arm. KCCQ-OSS improved by 4.1, 6.8, and 7.2 points respectively, versus 2.0 in the placebo arm. Treatment-emergent adverse events were similar across arms; hypotension occurred in 3% of the 160 mg arm and 1% of placebo.

**Conclusions.** Betapril produced a dose-dependent reduction in NT-proBNP and improvement in quality of life in HFpEF over 24 weeks. The 80 mg dose offered a favorable balance of efficacy and tolerability and is being carried forward into a Phase III outcomes trial.

## Methods

### Design
Phase II, multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group trial with four arms. The trial was registered prior to enrollment and conducted under Good Clinical Practice guidelines.

### Participants
Key inclusion criteria: age ≥ 50 years; LVEF ≥ 50% on qualifying echocardiogram; NYHA class II or III symptoms; NT-proBNP ≥ 300 pg/mL (≥ 600 pg/mL if atrial fibrillation present); stable background HFpEF therapy for at least 4 weeks. Key exclusions: eGFR < 30 mL/min/1.73m²; systolic BP < 100 mmHg; prior intolerance to natriuretic-peptide-potentiating agents.

### Interventions
Participants received oral betapril at 40 mg, 80 mg, 160 mg, or matching placebo, once daily for 24 weeks. Background HFpEF therapy (diuretics, SGLT2 inhibitors, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists) was continued at the discretion of the investigator.

### Outcomes
The primary endpoint was the ratio of NT-proBNP at week 24 compared to baseline, expressed as a geometric mean ratio versus placebo. Secondary endpoints included change in KCCQ Overall Summary Score (KCCQ-OSS), 6-minute walk distance, and safety.

## Results

### Enrollment
Between October 2023 and June 2024, 410 patients were screened and 284 randomized: 70 to betapril 40 mg, 72 to betapril 80 mg, 71 to betapril 160 mg, and 71 to placebo. Baseline characteristics were balanced across arms. Mean age was 68.2 years; 54% were female; 38% had atrial fibrillation; 47% had diabetes.

### Primary outcome
The geometric mean ratio of NT-proBNP at week 24 versus placebo was:
- Betapril 40 mg: 0.92 (95% CI 0.83-1.02)
- Betapril 80 mg: 0.81 (0.72-0.91)
- Betapril 160 mg: 0.78 (0.70-0.88)
Test for trend across dose P < 0.001.

### Secondary outcomes
KCCQ-OSS improved from baseline by 2.0 (placebo), 4.1 (40 mg), 6.8 (80 mg), and 7.2 (160 mg) points. 6-minute walk distance improved by 8, 18, 32, and 30 m across the four arms.

### Safety
Treatment-emergent adverse events occurred in 40-48% of participants across arms with no significant between-group difference. Clinically meaningful hypotension (systolic BP < 90 mmHg requiring intervention) occurred in 1% (placebo), 1% (40 mg), 3% (80 mg), and 3% (160 mg). No drug-related serious adverse events were reported.

## Discussion

In this Phase II dose-finding study, betapril produced a dose-dependent reduction in NT-proBNP and an improvement in patient-reported quality of life in HFpEF. The 80 mg dose achieved near-maximal efficacy on the primary endpoint and carried a lower burden of hypotension than the 160 mg dose, making it the preferred dose for subsequent Phase III evaluation.

Limitations include the modest sample size, the 24-week duration which does not permit assessment of hospitalization or mortality endpoints, and enrollment predominantly from academic centers.

## Conclusion

Betapril demonstrated a dose-dependent NT-proBNP reduction and quality-of-life improvement in HFpEF. The 80 mg once-daily dose is being advanced into a Phase III trial powered on cardiovascular death and heart-failure hospitalization.
